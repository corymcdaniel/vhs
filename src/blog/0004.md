# >> 0004 AI Projects: Using Claude to Master Your D&D Character

*What happens when you hand your character sheet to an AI and ask it to find everything you're missing*

## Komar

[dnd.corymcdaniel.com](https://dnd.corymcdaniel.com/)

I'm currently in an active D&D campaign playing **Komar** — a Halfling Rogue 4 (Arcane Trickster) with a Charlatan background. On paper, a rogue is straightforward: hide, stab, run. In practice, especially with the Arcane Trickster subclass, there are layers of synergies, edge cases, and rulebook interactions that take real effort to untangle.

I started using Claude to do that untangling for me — and it made a noticeable difference at the table.

### The Character

Komar is a small, sneaky con artist with an invisible magic hand and a bag full of ball bearings. Key stats: DEX +4, Stealth +8 (Expertise), Sneak Attack 2d6, one spell slot, and the Arcane Trickster's signature **Mage Hand Legerdemain** — an invisible hand that can pickpocket, disarm traps, and unlock doors from 30 feet away as a Bonus Action.

He also carries 980 ball bearings.

### How I Used Claude

The process started broad. I loaded Komar's full character sheet — pulled live from D&D Beyond — into a conversation and asked Claude to read the PHB 2024 rules and tell me what I was likely underusing.

That one question opened a lot of doors.

What came back wasn't just a summary of abilities. It was a breakdown of synergies I hadn't considered:

- **Catapult has only a Somatic component** — no verbal — which means casting it from hiding doesn't break stealth. I had been avoiding spells in stealth situations entirely.
- **Naturally Stealthy** lets Komar hide behind any Large or larger creature, not just environmental cover. Any Large ally on the field becomes usable concealment.
- **Vex property on the Shortbow** grants advantage on the *next* attack against the same target — which triggers Sneak Attack again in the same round. Two Sneak Attacks per round from range was something I hadn't been setting up deliberately.
- **Mage Hand Legerdemain** is a Bonus Action, meaning Komar can deploy the invisible hand *and* take a full attack action in the same turn. I had been treating it as an action.

From there I went deeper. I asked Claude to build out specific combat sequences — not just "what can Komar do" but "what does an optimal round look like and how do I set it up the turn before." That produced a set of named tactics I now actually reference: the Shadow Archer, the Ghost Hands Heist, the Ball Bearing Carpet, the Perfect Opening Ambush.

### The Most Useful Insight

The combo that changed how I think about Komar was the Silent Catapult Opener:

1. Hide before initiative (Stealth +8 — enemies need to beat DC 18 to find me)
2. Cast Catapult from hiding — 3d8 bludgeoning, no verbal component, Komar stays hidden
3. Shortbow Sneak Attack on the same target while still concealed

Average output: ~26 damage on a surprise round from a character enemies don't know is there. And because Catapult uses a Somatic-only casting, there's no verbal giveaway — something I never would have caught without reading the spell components carefully.

Claude caught it immediately.

### The Level Roadmap

I also asked Claude to look ahead. Level 5 is close (XP: 4,228 / 6,500) and it brings Uncanny Dodge and Sneak Attack bumping to 3d6. Level 7 unlocks Evasion and 2nd-level spells — Invisibility, Hold Person, Blur. Level 11 is Reliable Talent, which means proficient skills can never roll below 10. Komar's Stealth minimum becomes 18.

Knowing what's coming changed how I'm spending resources now. The one spell slot is precious — I'm preserving it for Charm Person or Grease in situations that warrant it, rather than burning it on Catapult when the bow handles damage fine.

### Why It Works

D&D rules are dense and cross-referenced. A single character at level 4 has interactions between class features, subclass features, species traits, feats, weapon properties, spell components, and equipment that would take hours to map out manually. Claude can cross-reference all of it instantly and answer questions like "does this work the way I think it does" or "what am I leaving on the table."

It's not replacing the fun of playing — it's removing the prep friction so more of the session is actual play and less is me re-reading spell descriptions mid-turn.

Komar is a significantly more dangerous Halfling because of it.
